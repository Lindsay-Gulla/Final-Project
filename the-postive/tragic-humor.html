“Humor is tragedy plus time" - Mark Twain

 

Despite all of the negativity, humor is an overall positive outlet. It can help people deal with tragic events; however, some jokes are taken too far and can be very offensive. There is a definite line between how and when a tragedy can be joked about.

Cindi May published in article in the Scientific American (2014) where she addresses the question, “When are Jokes about Tragedy Funny?” She analyzed different situations as well as a study conducted, which examined people’s reactions to jokes about Hurricane Sandy.

The jokes were presented to 100 participants at different points in time, from the day before the storm hit until 14 weeks after. Each participant was shown three different tweets from the twitter account called @HurricaneSandy. An example of a joke was, “@HurricaneSandy: JUS BLEW DA ROOF OFF A OLIVE GARDEN FREE BREADSTICKS 4 EVERYONE” (May, 2014, pp.1 ). What May gathered from the study is that tragedy does not get funnier over time; rather, they become humorous “with the passage of some time” (May, 2014, pp. 1).

The jokes were found most offensive two weeks after the disaster. The ratings peaked about a month after the event. What is interesting about May’s study is that jokes about disaster peak with a certain amount of time and then eventually they die out; people found the jokes less funny as time passed after the one month period.

Therefore, it can be inferred that jokes about tragedy are not funny when the wounds are still fresh; however, if the event is not fresh in the minds of the public, it is not offensive, but also it is found less funny. As May puts it, “we are able to appreciate the humor in tragic or devastating events after some delay because time reduces the severity of the violation.
