Plato

Beginning with one of the most influential thinkers in history, Plato, who was a major critic of humor, he argued that humor is an emotion that requires a lack of self-control and that laughter was an act of cruelty or scorn. In the Republic he advises those in power to avoid humor and laughter because “for ordinarily when one abandons himself to violent laughter, his condition provokes a violent reaction." In other words, Plato finds humor to be so malicious that he believes that laughter would cause a violent response from the state. He believes that humor; laughter and those who enjoy comedy are enjoying a type of evil. In Philebus he wrote, “The ridiculous is a certain kind of evil, specifically a vice.” By vice, Plato hints at the idea that humor is an indulgence of “self-ignorance: the people we laugh at imagine themselves to be wealthier, better looking, or more virtuous than they really are." By laughing at this person’s joke, we are giving into their self-ignorance.
