The Superiority Theory

The Superiority Theory is hinted at in Hobbes’ theory of humor. The theory says that laughter is an expression of superiority over others or even our past-selves.

This theory was weakened with Francis Hutcheson’s critique of Hobbes’ account of humor. Hutcheson argues that not all humor is a comparison to one’s self. He suggests that we laugh at figure of speeches, which does not include any type of human comparison. His counter argument is that pity is a feeling that is stronger than humor.

For example, if a man is driving a BMW down the road in the city and sees a homeless man begging for food, they may compare themselves to this person and think they are in a better situation, but it would not be amusing to this person. He says in these types of situations, “we are in greater danger of weeping than laughing." This is not the only situation in which the Superiority Theory could be challenged.

Not all cases of laughter involve the feeling of superiority, which means “sudden glory” is not the definition of laughter. For example, how can the laughter of a baby who is being tickled be explained with this theory?

Sometimes we laugh at someone who shows skills that we lack, for example, a football player that is a really good ballroom dancer. We would laugh because it would be unexpected, not because they we feel superior. Therefore, in this case, our self-comparison would not make us feel superior. There have been several times when I am on the phone with a person and I am searching for my phone, then I realize what I am doing and I tend to laugh.
